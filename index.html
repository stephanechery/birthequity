Executive Summary For Grant Reviewers

Need: Marion County, Indiana's most populous county (930,000+ residents), faces a maternal and infant health crisis concentrated among Black, low-income, and Medicaid-enrolled families. Black women in Indiana die from pregnancy-related causes at 3.1√ó the rate of white women. Marion County's preterm birth rate (14.1%) ranks among the state's worst. These disparities are not inevitable ‚Äî they reflect structural gaps in high-touch, culturally-aligned support during the perinatal period.

Approach: The Indiana Birth Equity Doula Collective (IBEDC) provides community-based doula services rooted in cultural humility, centering Black, Indigenous, Latina, and low-income birthing people. Our model pairs each client with a trained, certified community doula for 6‚Äì8 prenatal visits, continuous labor support, 6+ postpartum visits, lactation navigation, and social needs screening ‚Äî a proven, evidence-based intervention.

Evidence: Peer-reviewed literature demonstrates that doula support reduces C-sections by up to 40%, preterm births by 22%, and low birthweight by 31%. Medicaid managed care organizations (MCOs) increasingly view doulas as high-ROI members of the perinatal care team, with Indiana now pursuing Medicaid reimbursement pathways.

Workforce & Sustainability: IBEDC trains and employs community members ‚Äî prioritizing women from the communities most affected ‚Äî creating living-wage jobs while building a sustainable perinatal workforce pipeline. Revenue diversification across Medicaid MCO contracts, health system partnerships, foundation grants, and public health funding creates a resilient sustainability model with a 3-year path to 60% earned revenue.

Maternal Morbidity Drivers

Hemorrhage, hypertensive disorders, infections, and mental health crises account for 80%+ of preventable maternal deaths. Structural barriers ‚Äî implicit bias in clinical settings, delayed care-seeking, lack of care coordination ‚Äî amplify risk for Black women.

üë∂

Infant Health Disparities

Black infants in Marion Co. are 2.4√ó more likely to be born preterm and face NICU admission rates 35% higher than white infants. Average NICU stay: $3,500‚Äì$8,000/day. Early intervention and social support reduce these risks significantly.

üè•

Geographic Service Gaps

Marion County's east and northeast quadrants (highest Black/low-income populations) are effectively obstetric deserts post-2020 hospital consolidations. IU Health Methodist closed its labor unit in 2021; Eskenazi Health carries disproportionate safety-net load.

üß†

Perinatal Mental Health

1 in 5 birthing people experience perinatal mood and anxiety disorders (PMADs). Black women are less likely to be screened or referred. PHQ-9 and Edinburgh scales now standard ‚Äî doulas serve as a critical bridge to clinical care.

ü§±

Breastfeeding Disparities

Black infants are 20‚Äì30% less likely to be breastfed at 6 months. Lactation support ‚Äî a core doula service ‚Äî reduces this gap while lowering NICU readmission and respiratory illness rates.

üíä

Social Determinants of Risk

Housing instability, food insecurity, intimate partner violence, and lack of transportation are prevalent in high-risk ZIP codes. Doulas trained in social needs screening and community resource navigation address the upstream determinants clinical visits cannot.

Policy & Reimbursement Environment

Indiana's Medicaid reimbursement pathway for doulas is emerging but not yet finalized, creating both opportunity and strategic risk. This section maps the current landscape and compliance requirements.

Mechanism Status (Indiana, 2025) Relevant Detail Strategic Implication
Medicaid FFS Doula Billing In Development Indiana HB 1007 (2024) directed FSSA to study doula reimbursement. FSSA stakeholder process underway as of 2025. No active billing code yet. Monitor FSSA rulemaking; position for Day 1 enrollment. Peer states (MN, OR, VA) provide reimbursement models at $65‚Äì$115/visit.

Compliance Considerations

üìã

Scope of Practice

Indiana does not license doulas. Doulas provide emotional, physical, and informational support ‚Äî never clinical advice, diagnosis, or procedures. Clear scope-of-practice policies and training are essential.

üîí

HIPAA / Privacy

As a Business Associate or covered entity (if billing), IBEDC must implement HIPAA-compliant data practices. Community-based documentation using EHR-adjacent tools (e.g., Unite Us, Aunt Bertha) requires careful data-sharing agreements.

üìù

Documentation Standards

MCOs and health system partners will require standardized visit documentation, outcome data capture, and possibly EHR integration. Invest early in EMR-compatible charting tools.

üë•

Supervision Models

Doulas are not licensed practitioners; supervision by a clinical partner (midwife, CNM, or OB) through a formal MOU adds credibility, supports MCO contracting, and creates a quality assurance framework.

üåê Framework Analysis

PESTEL: External Environment

Framework rationale: PESTEL surfaces macro-level forces shaping the operating environment for a community health organization. Combined with SWOT (organizational positioning) and an ecosystem map (relational/financial flows), these three frameworks together provide a complete strategic picture for a doula collective at this stage.

P

Political

  • Republican-controlled Indiana legislature supportive of workforce development; cautious on new Medicaid spending
  • HB 1007 (2024) directs FSSA to study doula reimbursement ‚Äî bipartisan opportunity
  • Federal maternal health equity priorities (CMS TMaH, HRSA Healthy Start) provide tailwind regardless of state politics
  • Post-Dobbs policy environment may reduce some reproductive health funding; abortion-adjacent framing should be avoided in state-facing communications
  • Marion County (Indy) is a Democratic-leaning urban county; city government supports health equity initiatives (MCHD, Indy Equity Fund)
E

Economic

  • Indiana Medicaid (Healthy Indiana Plan) covers ~60% of Marion Co. births; expanding postpartum eligibility (12 months) grows addressable market
  • MCO medical loss ratio rules incentivize value-based contracting with community organizations showing cost savings
  • Nonprofit labor market tight ‚Äî doula workforce competes with CHW and home visiting roles for bilingual, community-rooted talent
  • Philanthropic environment positive: Lilly Endowment, RWJF, and health system community benefit funds are active in health equity
  • Grant inflation ‚Äî cost per outcome must remain competitive with home visiting programs (~$4,500‚Äì$6,000/family/year)
S

Social

  • Growing awareness of racism in obstetric care; "birth equity" is a recognized movement with community and funder resonance
  • Black community-led organizations have established trust where hospital systems have failed
  • Generational trauma and medical mistrust among Black and Indigenous families creates demand for culturally-aligned support
  • Social media awareness of doula benefits growing rapidly among Millennial/Gen Z birthing people
  • Hispanic/Latina population in Marion Co. is growing; Spanish-language and bilingual doula capacity is a differentiator
T

Technological

  • Telehealth expansion post-COVID enables remote prenatal check-ins and reduces access barriers
  • EHR integration (Epic, Cerner) increasingly expected by hospital partners and MCOs for documentation and outcomes tracking
  • Social needs screening platforms (Unite Us, Findhelp/Aunt Bertha) enable warm referrals and close the loop on SDOH interventions
  • Remote fetal monitoring tools may expand doula role in high-risk monitoring with appropriate training
  • Data collection for MCO outcomes reporting requires investment in CRM / case management software
E

Environmental

  • Environmental justice lens: air quality, heat, and water quality in East Indianapolis disproportionately affect pregnancy outcomes
  • Climate-related extreme heat events pose rising risk for pregnant persons in low-income housing without AC
  • Transportation access remains critical ‚Äî Indy public transit gaps require vehicle mileage reimbursement policies for doulas
  • Opportunity to incorporate environmental health education into doula training curriculum
L

Legal

  • No Indiana law prohibiting or licensing doulas ‚Äî legal operating environment permissive
  • HIPAA and 42 CFR Part 2 (if serving clients with SUD histories) govern data sharing ‚Äî requires legal review of MOUs
  • Nonprofit corporate governance and 501(c)(3) compliance requirements for grant receipt
  • Employment law: doula classification as employee vs. independent contractor has tax and liability implications; employee model preferred for MCO contracting
  • Emerging state licensure discussions in some states ‚Äî monitor for potential Indiana action

Program Model & Theory of Change

IBEDC's doula service model delivers high-touch, culturally-responsive support across the full perinatal continuum ‚Äî from early pregnancy through 12 months postpartum.

ü§ù

Prenatal Support (6‚Äì8 visits)

Birth plan development, care navigation, SDOH screening (housing, food, DV, transportation), perinatal mental health check-ins, and provider communication support.

üë∂

Labor & Birth Support

Continuous one-on-one doula presence during labor and delivery. Physical comfort measures, emotional support, advocacy, and birth partner coaching. Reduces C-sections, epidurals, and NICU admissions.

üå∏

Postpartum Support (6+ visits over 12 weeks)

Edinburgh PMAD screening, breastfeeding support and lactation navigation, newborn care education, family bonding support, community resource connection, and referral to mental health services.
